Early Warning System for Sustained Market Downturns

The dashboard combines two complementary statistical models — a Hidden Markov Model (HMM) and a Bayesian Online Changepoint Detector (BOCPD) — to distinguish persistent bear and crisis regimes from ordinary volatility. This note explains how each layer works and what the dashboard signals mean.

The two core models
Layer 1 · Regime Identification

Hidden Markov Model

What market state are we in right now?

Financial markets don't move randomly — they cycle through distinct regimes that differ in trend, volatility and drawdown depth. The HMM learns these regimes from history and, given today's data, infers which one we are most likely in.

How it works: The model treats the true market regime as a hidden variable that cannot be observed directly. It observes three daily signals from the S&P 500 — the day's return, 21-day rolling volatility, and how far prices sit below their one-year high — and uses these to estimate the probability of three hidden states:

Crisis  ·  vol ≈ 26%, drawdown ≈ −17% Bear  ·  vol ≈ 13%, drawdown ≈ −3% Bull  ·  vol ≈ 9%, drawdown ≈ −1%

Persistence filter: Raw HMM posteriors can flicker day-to-day. A 20-day rolling average and a minimum-duration rule mean the model only declares a regime change when the evidence is sustained — not on a single bad session. Typical regimes last 54–166 trading days.

Dashboard output: The current regime label (Bull / Bear / Crisis) and P(Crisis) — the probability the market is in a crisis state. P(Crisis) feeds directly into the risk score even when the headline label shows Bear.

Layer 2 · Early Warning

Bayesian Changepoint Detector

Are cross-market relationships breaking down?

Major downturns rarely arrive without warning in the underlying structure of markets. Correlations between equity indices shift, the equity–rates relationship inverts as capital flees to safety, and volatility regimes change — often weeks before index levels reflect the stress.

How it works: BOCPD monitors 22 cross-asset series simultaneously: pairwise correlations across US, UK, Japanese and Hong Kong equities, per-index volatility series, and equity/rates cross-correlations that capture flight-to-safety behaviour. For each series it maintains a probability distribution over how long the current statistical regime has been running. When that distribution collapses to a very short run-length, a structural break has been detected.

Why it leads: Structural deterioration in cross-market relationships precedes price falls because it reflects positioning and risk-appetite shifts before they are fully priced in. Validated lead times over four major crises:

EventBOCPD lead vs HMM
Dot-com (2000)43 days
Global Financial Crisis (2007)28 days
Rate hike cycle (2022)109 days

Dashboard output: Daily break probability and a 30-day break frequency count. Frequency is more stable than the daily spike and is one of the top five features in the ensemble.

Combining the signals — the risk score

An XGBoost ensemble combines the HMM and BOCPD outputs with macro features (volatility, drawdown, credit spreads) into a single risk score between 0 and 1. The score is trained to predict whether the S&P 500 will fall more than 10% within the next 20 trading days. All inputs are lagged by one day to prevent any look-ahead bias.

Two versions of the score are produced. The raw score moves continuously day-to-day and is used for alerts and trend tracking. A calibrated score converts it to an approximate probability statement (e.g. "~13% chance of a −10% drawdown in the next 20 days") and is more suitable for stakeholder communication, though it can remain flat for several days when conditions are stable. Alert flags are raised at the 90th and 95th historical percentile of the raw score.

Reading the dashboard
Alert Banner

Green / Amber / Red based on whether the raw risk score exceeds the p90 or p95 threshold. No alert does not mean no risk — it means risk is below the historical top 10%.

KPI Cards

Five key metrics with day-on-day deltas: current regime, P(Crisis), BOCPD 30-day break count, raw risk score, and calibrated probability.

Risk Score Chart

Three-year history of the raw score with p90/p95 threshold lines overlaid. The colour bar beneath shows the HMM regime at each point in time.

Signal Breakdown

90-day view of the three component signals — P(Crisis), BOCPD break probability, and risk score — so you can see which layer is driving any elevated reading.

History Table

Eight-week rolling table of all key outputs. Useful for spotting gradual trend changes that are not yet above alert thresholds.

Important caveat

The model detects slow-building structural deterioration. Pure exogenous shocks (e.g. COVID-19) produce no build-up signal and will not trigger an early warning.
